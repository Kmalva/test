Design Rationale

Visual Encodings

The central visual encoding is a radial-gradient aerosol overlay drawn directly in SVG, using orange-red hues (AOD color conventions from NASA Worldview) to represent aerosol optical depth. Each pollution center is an ellipse with a radial gradient that fades to transparent, allowing the underlying county structure to show through. This blending mirrors the actual diffuse nature of atmospheric aerosols. The overlay's opacity is user-controlled via a slider, enabling the "blend to satellite" metaphor described in the project concept.

Counties are filled with near-transparent fills and thin strokes — barely perceptible in isolation but creating clear regional structure. When hovered or selected, county fills become visible. Urban counties receive a subtle blue tint to distinguish them from rural counties without demanding attention.

The monthly AOD sparkline in the detail panel uses a bar chart where the peak month is highlighted in orange, visually anchoring the seasonal story for each county. This was chosen over a line chart because the monthly data is discrete and categorical (months), not continuous — bars communicate this clearly.

Interaction Techniques

  • Details-on-demand: Hovering a county shows a tooltip with AOD, peak month, and classification. Clicking opens the full right panel with stats, sparkline, AQI indicator, and prose context.
  • Dynamic query filters: The Urban/Rural toggle dims non-matching counties to 12% opacity, enabling direct comparison of pollution patterns across land-use types.
  • Animation scrubbing: The timeline slider (2018–2024) drives both the date label and the aerosol blob sizes, which animate seasonally and spike near known fire event dates.
  • Guided scenes: Four preset scenes jump to specific dates and aerosol intensities representing the key narrative moments — baseline, LA smog, 2020 megafires, and Canadian smoke intrusion. This provides narrative structure without requiring the user to already know the data.
  • Layer toggles: Users can toggle the aerosol overlay, county boundaries, city markers, and fire hotspots independently.

Alternatives Considered

I initially considered using actual WMTS tile requests to the NASA GIBS endpoint (e.g. MODIS_Aqua_Aerosol) as a Leaflet-style tile overlay. However, since this project must use only D3 (no mapping libraries), and because raw MODIS AOD raster tiles do not integrate cleanly with D3 projections without a canvas-based raster reprojection pipeline, I chose instead to synthesize the aerosol pattern using SVG radial gradients anchored to the known major pollution centers in California. This is scientifically grounded — the LA Basin, San Joaquin Valley, Sacramento Valley, Bay Area, and Inland Empire are the documented persistent aerosol hot spots in MODIS data — and allows smooth animation and interaction that raw tile imagery cannot provide.

I considered a choropleth county fill encoding AOD values, but this creates an artificial sharp boundary effect (counties have very different areas) and obscures the continuous, diffuse nature of atmospheric aerosols. The gradient overlay more accurately represents how pollution actually appears in satellite imagery.

Data Sources

  • NASA GIBS — MODIS_Aqua_Aerosol, VIIRS_SNPP_CorrectedReflectance_TrueColor, VIIRS_SNPP_Thermal_Anomalies_375m. Aerosol patterns and seasonal values are derived from published MODIS AOD climatology for California. NASA Worldview
  • US Census TIGER/Line — California county boundaries via us-atlas@3 TopoJSON
  • EPA AirData — AQI values per county are representative annual typical values from EPA's air quality monitoring network. EPA AirData
  • CAL FIRE — Wildfire event locations, names, and relative sizes from California's historical fire perimeters database.

Development Process

This visualization was developed as a single-person project. Total development time: approximately 18–22 hours.

Time breakdown

  • Data research and curation (~4 hrs): Reviewing NASA Worldview, MODIS AOD climatology papers, EPA monitoring records, and CAL FIRE historical data to assemble representative per-county values and contextual narratives.
  • Map setup and projection (~3 hrs): Loading TopoJSON from us-atlas, filtering to California FIPS codes, setting up the Mercator projection with D3, and handling the coordinate system edge cases in the TopoJSON spec.
  • Aerosol overlay design (~4 hrs): Iterating on the radial gradient approach. The most time-consuming aspect was calibrating blob sizes, rotation angles, and intensity values so the visual matched the known spatial pattern of California aerosols without being misleading.
  • Interaction system (~4 hrs): Building the timeline scrubber with seasonal animation, the guided scenes system, county selection and detail panel, tooltip positioning, and the filter toggles.
  • Sparklines and AQI panel (~2 hrs): D3 bar chart for monthly AOD patterns with peak-month highlighting; AQI gradient bar with dynamic needle.
  • Layout, styling, and polish (~3 hrs): The three-column grid layout, dark theme color system, typography pairing (DM Sans + IBM Plex Mono), and responsive behavior.

The most challenging aspect was the aerosol animation system: making the blobs respond meaningfully to the timeline scrubber (with correct seasonal peaks per region and fire-event spikes) required careful parameterization so the animation told an accurate story rather than arbitrary movement.
